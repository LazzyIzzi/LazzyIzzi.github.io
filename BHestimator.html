Beam hardening is the shifting of the broad spectrum of a conventional x-ray source toward higher average (hard) energies as it passes through filters, samples and detectors. This results in an over-estimate of attenuation in thinner portions of the sample.  It produces the well-known cupping artifact in tomographic images.

The ImageJ Beam Hardening Estimator plugin is built upon the Mu Mass Calculator library.  It provides an interactive way to estimate the severity of beam hardening artifact when acquiring radiographs or tomography data.

The above figure shows a schematic of a simple radiography or CT scanner. The arrow colors correspond to the plot colors(below), the arrow widths indicate spectral width and the length indicates intensity.

This figure shows the user dialog(left) and two windows(right) that are created or updated when the Update Plot button is clicked, (top) a plot of intensity vs X-ray energy at five sampling points along the beam path, and (bottom) the report issued for each trial configuration. The dialog values should be clear to persons familiar with conventional X-ray sources with the exception of the Formula which is entered as atom1:count1:atom2:count2... as required by MuMassCalculator.

Simple thresholding of tomographic reconstructions typically requires less than 5% beam hardening over the entire image to successfully segment the components.  This may vary depending on materials contrast. The goal of this plugin is to quickly select a source, filter, and detector configuration that minimizes beam hardening while optimizing contrast and count rate using the sample attenuation≅2 criterion .  A user makes appropriate selections in the non-modal dialog and clicks the Update Plot button.  The spectral dependencies are displayed and the report is updated with the configuration and beam hardening metrics.

Special Note:  This is NOT a detective quantum efficiency (DQE) calculator, the detector is modeled as ideal with the exception of a correction for photons that pass through the scintillator without being detected. Real detectors vary considerably in design, construction and response.
